7 Rules

ClueMaker can also be used to write rules. Rules can be used to override the probabilistic decision in special cases. The syntax of a rule is similar to that of a clue. The keyword rule is used in place of clue. In addition to differ, match, and hold, rules can use one of the pseudo decisions nomatch, nodiffer, nohold, and none.

If a rule with decision differ, match, or hold fires on a pair, the ChoiceMaker decision is differ, match, or hold, respectively, independent of the probabilistic decision (the decision based on the match probability and the thresholds). If a rule with pseudo decision nomatch fires on a pair, the ChoiceMaker decision is hold if the probabilistic decision is hold or match and differ otherwise. If a rule with pseudo decision nodiffer fires on a pair, the ChoiceMaker decision is hold if the probabilistic decision is hold or differ and match otherwise. If a rule with pseudo decision nohold fires on a pair, the ChoiceMaker decision is differ if the probabilistic decision is hold or differ and match otherwise.

A rule with pseudo decision none does not affect or reinforce the decision. It is meant to be used in conjunction with one of the modifiers note or report for reporting without affecting the decision or for selection of pairs in ChoiceMaker ModelMaker.

If multiple rules with decisions other than none activate on a given record pair, we distinguish two cases. If all rules with decisions other than none produce the same decision, then this consensus decision is the final decision. Otherwise, the final decision is hold.

All rules must have decisions and be of type boolean, regardless of the type of the clue set.

Rules can also be used as (temporary) replacements of decisive clues for which not enough supporting training data is available. For example, we might consider one person having first name `TWIN-A' and the other `TWIN-B' a strong indication that they are not the same person. Yet, our training data might not contain any, or just very few such cases. Thus, it is impossible to train such a clue-at least until more training data becomes available. We can, however, create a nomatch rule with the corresponding predicate.

If the business rules corresponding to the rules have been used to human mark the pairs, it is advisable to add a differ clue with the same expression for each differ rule and vice versa for match clues. Additionally, differ clues may be introduced for nomatch rules and match clues for nodiffer clues. This may produce a more accurate probabilistic model. Alternatively, pairs on which rules fire may be removed from the training source
