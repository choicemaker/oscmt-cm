1 Maximum Entropy

The maximum entropy plugins com.choicemaker.cm.ml.me.base and com.choicemaker.cm.ml.me.gui provide support for maximum entropy.

1.1 Introduction

Maximum entropy assigns a weight to each clue. The weight expresses the relative importance of a clue; higher weights mean more important. The match-probability for a given record pair is computed using the clue-weights as shown in Figure 62.

Clues with weights below 1 are negatively correlated. For example, a match clue with a weight of 0.8 actually reduces the match probability of a pair when it fires.

1.2 Strengths and weaknesses

Maximum entropy has the following strengths:

  • Relatively easy to understand.
  • Weights give a good intuition of the relative importance of clues.
  • Weights below 1 give a good indication of clues that don't work.
  • Fast training and run-time execution.

Maximum entropy has the following weaknesses:

  • May require compound clues for optimal performance. Compound clues are clues that incorporate other clues. For instance, same(r.firstName) && same(r.lastName) && same(r.birthday).
  • May require multi-step decision. E.g., it may be necessary to first make a decision whether two persons are in the same household by comparing the addresses. Only if this is the case, a second decision is made whether the two records denote the same person in the household by comparing the first names and dates of birth. Other techniques give the same high accuracy without requiring a multi-step decision.
1.3 ModelMaker GUI

Figure 63 shows the ModelMaker training dialog with maximum entropy selected as machine learning technique. The upper part of the dialog, which is independent of the machine learning technique, is documented in Chapter II.

The only element that is specific to maximum entropy is the number of training iterations. This defines the maximum number of iterations of the maximum entropy training algorithm for computing the weights. The ideal value depends on the model and the training data. Typically, a value of 6,000 is suitable for production models. During initial training, values as low as 1,000 may give sufficiently accurate results.

Higher values usually give better results, but take more time. Excessively high values, e.g., greater than 10,000 may lead to a loss of accuracy because of overtraining or numeric errors.
