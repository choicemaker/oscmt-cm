3.1.1.1 Decisions

Clues come in three varieties - match-clues which predict match, differ-clues which predict differ, and hold-clues which predict hold. Most models have only match- and differ-clues.

When a match-clue fires it means that according to that clue, the records match. Similarly when a differ-clue fires it means that according to that clue, the records differ. When a match-clue fires on a record pair that is human-marked match, we call it a hit. When a match-clue fires on a record pair that is marked differ or hold, we call it a miss. Good clues are usually right (hit) more often than they're wrong (miss). Thus when applied over the whole marked record pair source, we look for the number of hits to be greater than the number of misses. Clues that do not exhibit this behavior are suspect and should be carefully checked for errors, but not necessarily thrown out.

Note also that when a match-clue fails to fire on a record-pair, it in no way indicates that the pairs match or differ. It only means that the conditions required for the application of the particular clue on that pair did not hold. Among these conditions one usually includes requirements for the fields to contain valid data. Such validity conditions are defined in the schema. All the validity checks and other conditions that must be satisfied for a clue to fire are coded as part of the clue using the ClueMaker language.

Not all machine learning techniques consider the decision of the clue. Some simply consider whether it fires or not.
